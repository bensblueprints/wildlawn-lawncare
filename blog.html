Maintaining a beautiful lawn in the Atlanta metro area presents unique challenges that homeowners in other parts of the country simply do not face. Between the humid subtropical climate, the clay-heavy soil, and the variety of warm-season and cool-season grasses that grow here, lawn care tips for Atlanta homeowners need to be tailored to local conditions. Whether you live in Marietta, Roswell, Kennesaw, or anywhere else in Metro Atlanta, this comprehensive guide will give you the knowledge you need to keep your yard looking its absolute best year-round.

At Wild Lawn, we have spent years caring for lawns across the greater Atlanta area, and we have learned that the difference between a struggling yard and a thriving one often comes down to understanding a few fundamental principles. Lawn maintenance in Georgia is not the same as lawn maintenance in Ohio or California, and the sooner you adapt your approach to our specific climate, the better your results will be.

Understanding Your Atlanta Lawn

The first step toward better lawn care in Atlanta is understanding what you are working with. Metro Atlanta sits in USDA Hardiness Zone 7b to 8a, which means we experience hot, humid summers and relatively mild winters. This climate zone is what makes our region part of the "transition zone" for turfgrass, meaning both warm-season and cool-season grasses can potentially survive here, though each comes with trade-offs.

The most common grass types you will find in Marietta, Roswell, and Kennesaw lawns include:

  • Bermuda Grass: The workhorse of Southern lawns. Bermuda thrives in full sun and can handle the intense Georgia summer heat. It spreads aggressively through stolons and rhizomes, which helps it recover from damage quickly. Most athletic fields and golf courses in Metro Atlanta rely on Bermuda grass for this reason.
  • Zoysia Grass: A dense, carpet-like grass that is increasingly popular in the Atlanta suburbs. Zoysia is more shade-tolerant than Bermuda and creates a lush, thick lawn that naturally crowds out weeds. The trade-off is that it grows more slowly and can take longer to establish.
  • Tall Fescue: The dominant cool-season grass in our transition zone. Fescue stays green during the cooler months when warm-season grasses go dormant, but it struggles in the peak of summer. Many homeowners in shaded neighborhoods throughout Roswell and Marietta rely on fescue because it handles partial shade well.
  • St. Augustine Grass: Less common than the others in Metro Atlanta, but you will still find it in some established neighborhoods. St. Augustine offers excellent shade tolerance and creates a thick, dark green lawn, but it is less cold-hardy than Bermuda or Zoysia.

Understanding which grass you have is the foundation of effective lawn care. Every mowing height, fertilizer schedule, and watering recommendation depends on your specific turf type. If you are not sure what grass you have, take a close-up photo and bring it to your local extension office, or simply ask your lawn care professional during your next service.

Beyond the grass itself, you need to understand your soil. Most of Metro Atlanta sits on heavy red clay, which is notoriously difficult for grass roots. Clay soil compacts easily, drains poorly when saturated, and becomes rock-hard during dry spells. This is why aeration is so important for Atlanta lawns, but we will get to that later. A simple soil test through the University of Georgia Extension Service costs around fifteen dollars and will tell you your soil pH, nutrient levels, and specific amendment recommendations.

Mowing Like a Pro

Mowing is the single most frequent lawn care activity, and it is also the one that most homeowners get wrong. Proper mowing technique can be the difference between a lawn that looks professionally maintained and one that always seems to struggle. Here are the lawn care tips that professionals in Atlanta follow every time they mow.

The most important rule is mowing at the correct height for your grass type:

  • Bermuda Grass: Mow at 1 to 1.5 inches. Bermuda performs best when kept relatively short, which encourages lateral spreading and creates that dense, carpet-like appearance. If you let Bermuda grow too tall, it can develop thatch problems and become leggy.
  • Zoysia Grass: Mow at 1.5 to 2.5 inches. Zoysia tolerates a slightly higher cut than Bermuda and actually benefits from a bit more leaf blade to support its slower growth habit.
  • Tall Fescue: Mow at 3 to 4 inches. This is where many Atlanta homeowners make a critical mistake. Fescue needs to be kept tall, especially during summer, because the longer leaf blades shade the soil, keep the root zone cooler, and reduce water evaporation. Cutting fescue too short during a Georgia summer is one of the fastest ways to kill your lawn.

Always follow the one-third rule: never remove more than one-third of the grass blade in a single mowing. If your Bermuda lawn is at two inches and you want it at one inch, do not cut it all the way down in one pass. Instead, bring it down gradually over two or three mowings. Removing too much at once shocks the plant, weakens the root system, and leaves the lawn vulnerable to disease and weed invasion.

Keep your mower blades sharp. Dull blades tear the grass rather than cutting it cleanly, which creates ragged edges that turn brown and invite fungal diseases. Professional lawn care companies in Marietta and Kennesaw sharpen their blades after every eight to ten hours of mowing, and homeowners should sharpen theirs at least two to three times per season.

Finally, alternate your mowing pattern each time. If you mowed north-south last week, mow east-west this week. Alternating patterns prevents the grass from developing a grain or lean in one direction and promotes more upright, even growth.

Watering Wisely in Georgia's Climate

Atlanta receives an average of about 50 inches of rainfall per year, which is actually generous compared to many parts of the country. However, that rainfall is not evenly distributed. We typically see heavy spring rains, sporadic summer thunderstorms with dry stretches in between, and drier fall periods. Understanding this pattern is essential for watering wisely in Georgia's climate.

The general rule for Atlanta lawns is that they need about one inch of water per week, including rainfall. During the hottest months of July and August, this may increase to one and a half inches per week, especially for fescue lawns that struggle with heat stress.

Here are the watering principles that guide effective lawn maintenance in Georgia:

  • Water deeply and infrequently. Two to three deep watering sessions per week are far better than light daily watering. Deep watering encourages roots to grow deeper into the soil, making the lawn more drought-tolerant. Light daily watering keeps roots shallow and dependent on constant moisture.
  • Water early in the morning. The ideal time is between 5 a.m. and 9 a.m. This gives the grass time to absorb the water before the heat of the day causes evaporation, and it ensures the leaf blades dry before evening, which reduces the risk of fungal diseases. Evening watering is one of the leading causes of lawn fungus in the humid Atlanta climate.
  • Pay attention to water restrictions. Metro Atlanta periodically implements outdoor watering restrictions, especially during drought conditions. Cobb County, Fulton County, and Cherokee County each have their own water use ordinances, so make sure you know the rules in your area.
  • Use the tuna can test. Place several empty tuna cans across your lawn before running your sprinklers. When the cans have about one inch of water in them, you know how long it takes your system to deliver an inch of irrigation. This simple test eliminates guesswork.

One of the biggest mistakes we see in Marietta and Roswell is overwatering. Homeowners often set their irrigation systems to run daily, which not only wastes water but creates the saturated conditions that promote fungal diseases like brown patch and dollar spot. If your lawn is squelchy or you notice mushrooms popping up frequently, you are very likely overwatering.

Fertilization Timing for Georgia

Fertilization is where knowing your grass type becomes absolutely critical, because warm-season and cool-season grasses need to be fertilized on completely different schedules. Getting this wrong can actually harm your lawn rather than help it.

For Bermuda and Zoysia (warm-season grasses):

  • First application (April-May): Apply a balanced fertilizer once the grass has fully greened up and begun active growth. In the Atlanta area, this typically happens by mid-April for Bermuda and late April for Zoysia.
  • Second application (June): A nitrogen-heavy fertilizer to fuel the peak growing season.
  • Third application (August): Another nitrogen application to carry the lawn through the last stretch of summer.
  • Final application (September-October): A potassium-rich winterizer to help the grass store energy before going dormant.

For Tall Fescue (cool-season grass):

  • Primary application (September-October): This is the most important fertilization of the year for fescue. Fall is when fescue is actively growing and can best utilize nutrients.
  • Second application (November): A winterizer application that feeds the roots through the cooler months.
  • Spring application (March): A light application to support spring growth, but be careful not to over-fertilize in spring, as this can lead to excessive top growth that makes the grass more vulnerable to summer heat.
  • Avoid summer fertilization: Never fertilize fescue during June, July, or August. The grass is already under heat stress, and fertilizer will push it to grow when it should be conserving energy.

Always have a soil test done before creating a fertilization plan. The results will tell you exactly what nutrients your soil needs and, more importantly, what it does not need. Over-fertilization is a common problem that leads to excessive growth, thatch buildup, and environmental issues from nutrient runoff.

Weed Prevention Strategies

In Metro Atlanta, you will battle a familiar cast of weed villains: crabgrass, dallisgrass, nutsedge, clover, dandelions, chickweed, and the seemingly indestructible Chamberbitter. The best weed prevention strategy is a thick, healthy lawn that naturally crowds out weeds, but even the best lawns need some targeted intervention.

Pre-emergent herbicides are your first line of defense. These products create a chemical barrier in the soil that prevents weed seeds from germinating. For Atlanta lawns, the timing is critical:

  • Apply the first round of pre-emergent in late February to early March, before soil temperatures consistently reach 55 degrees Fahrenheit. This targets crabgrass and other summer annual weeds.
  • Apply a second round in mid-September to early October to prevent winter annual weeds like chickweed, henbit, and annual bluegrass.

A common mistake is applying pre-emergent too late. By the time you actually see crabgrass in your lawn, the pre-emergent window has already passed. Timing the application to soil temperature rather than calendar dates gives you the best results. Many homeowners in Kennesaw and Roswell rely on their lawn care provider to handle this timing precisely.

Post-emergent herbicides are used to kill weeds that are already growing. Select products carefully based on the specific weed you are targeting and the type of grass you have. A product that is safe for Bermuda lawns might kill fescue, and vice versa. When in doubt, spot-treat individual weeds rather than broadcasting herbicide across the entire lawn.

For stubborn perennial weeds like nutsedge, which thrives in Atlanta's humid climate, you may need a specialized herbicide containing halosulfuron or sulfentrazone. Nutsedge is not actually a grass but a sedge, so standard broadleaf or grassy weed killers will not touch it.

Seasonal Lawn Care Checklist

Here is your complete seasonal lawn care checklist for Atlanta, Georgia:

Spring (March - May)

  • Apply pre-emergent herbicide by early March
  • Schedule a soil test if you have not done one in the past two years
  • Begin mowing warm-season grasses once they start greening up
  • Sharpen mower blades
  • Inspect and start up irrigation systems
  • Apply first fertilizer to warm-season lawns (mid-April to May)
  • Address any bare patches with sod or seed (warm-season: sod; fescue: wait until fall to seed)
  • Clean up leaves and debris from winter

Summer (June - August)

  • Mow regularly at the proper height for your grass type
  • Water deeply two to three times per week during dry spells
  • Treat active weed problems with post-emergent herbicides
  • Watch for signs of fungal disease (brown patch, dollar spot)
  • Apply summer fertilizer to Bermuda and Zoysia lawns
  • Raise mowing height on fescue to 4 inches to combat heat stress
  • Avoid heavy foot traffic on fescue during extreme heat

Fall (September - November)

  • Apply fall pre-emergent for winter weeds
  • Overseed fescue lawns in mid-September to mid-October
  • Aerate compacted lawns (core aeration is best)
  • Apply fall fertilizer to all grass types
  • Continue mowing until grass goes dormant
  • Remove fallen leaves promptly to prevent smothering
  • Apply lime if soil test indicates low pH

Winter (December - February)

  • Keep fallen leaves off the lawn
  • Avoid walking on frozen grass
  • Service and maintain lawn equipment
  • Plan your spring lawn care strategy
  • Watch for winter weeds and treat if necessary
  • Leave warm-season grasses alone during dormancy

How Often Should You Mow Your Lawn in Georgia?

One of the most common questions we hear from homeowners across Metro Atlanta is, "How often should I mow my lawn?" It sounds like a simple question, but the answer depends on several factors that are specific to living in Georgia. Your lawn mowing frequency in Atlanta is determined by your grass type, the time of year, recent weather conditions, and your desired lawn appearance. Getting the mowing schedule right for your Georgia lawn is one of the most impactful things you can do for your yard's health and appearance.

Unlike homeowners in more temperate climates who deal with one relatively uniform growing season, Georgia residents manage lawns that behave very differently depending on the month. Some grasses explode with growth in July while barely growing at all in January. Others peak in October but struggle through August. Understanding these patterns is the key to developing a mowing schedule in Georgia that keeps your lawn looking its best without stressing the turf.

Identifying Your Grass Type

Before you can establish the right mowing frequency for your lawn, you need to know exactly what type of grass you are working with. Metro Atlanta is home to several common turfgrass species, and each one has distinct growth habits that directly influence how often it needs to be mowed.

Bermuda Grass

Bermuda grass is by far the most common warm-season turf in the Atlanta metro area. You will find it everywhere from the subdivisions of Alpharetta to the established neighborhoods of Smyrna. Bermuda is identifiable by its fine-textured blades, dense growth pattern, and aggressive spreading habit through both stolons (above-ground runners) and rhizomes (below-ground runners). The color is a medium to dark green during the growing season, and it turns completely tan or brown once it goes dormant in late fall.

What makes Bermuda unique is its aggressive growth rate. During the peak of a Georgia summer, a well-fertilized Bermuda lawn can grow one to two inches per week or even more. This is the grass that will have you mowing twice a week if you let your schedule slip. Bermuda is at its healthiest when maintained at a relatively short height, which means frequent mowing is not just cosmetic but essential for the turf's vitality.

Bermuda thrives in full sun and performs poorly in shade. If your property in Woodstock or Alpharetta has heavy tree cover, Bermuda is probably struggling in those shaded areas, and you may want to consider a more shade-tolerant alternative for those spots.

Zoysia Grass

Zoysia has been gaining popularity across Metro Atlanta for good reason. It creates a dense, luxurious lawn that feels like a thick carpet underfoot. Zoysia blades are wider than Bermuda and have a slightly coarser texture. The color is a rich, deep green, and the density of the turf naturally resists weed invasion better than most other grass types.

Zoysia grows more slowly than Bermuda, which is both an advantage and a disadvantage. On the positive side, you will mow less frequently. On the negative side, Zoysia is slower to establish, slower to recover from damage, and slower to fill in bare patches. During peak growing season, Zoysia typically needs mowing every seven to ten days rather than the every-five-days schedule that Bermuda demands.

Zoysia tolerates moderate shade, making it an excellent choice for properties in Smyrna and Woodstock that have mature trees. It also handles foot traffic well once established, making it popular for families with active children and pets.

Tall Fescue

Tall fescue is the go-to cool-season grass for Metro Atlanta. You will recognize it by its wider, coarser blade compared to Bermuda, its bunch-type growth habit (it grows in clumps rather than spreading through runners), and its ability to stay green well into the fall and through mild winters. Many homeowners in shaded neighborhoods throughout Alpharetta and Smyrna rely on fescue because it handles partial shade far better than any warm-season option.

Fescue grows most actively in spring and fall when temperatures are between 60 and 75 degrees Fahrenheit. During these periods, it may need weekly mowing. In summer, fescue growth slows dramatically and may go semi-dormant during extreme heat, potentially requiring mowing only every two to three weeks. The critical thing to remember is that fescue should always be mowed at a taller height than warm-season grasses to protect its crowns from heat stress.

St. Augustine Grass

While less common in Metro Atlanta than the other three, St. Augustine deserves mention because you will still encounter it in some established neighborhoods. It is identifiable by its very wide, flat blades and its dark blue-green color. St. Augustine spreads through stolons and creates a thick, dense mat that looks impressive when well maintained.

St. Augustine has a moderate growth rate and typically needs mowing every seven to ten days during the growing season. It is the most shade-tolerant of the warm-season grasses, but it is also the least cold-hardy, which makes it a risky choice in parts of Metro Atlanta that experience harder freezes. During the growing season, St. Augustine should be maintained at 3 to 4 inches in height.

Mowing Frequency by Season

Understanding how your lawn mowing frequency changes with the seasons is essential for Georgia homeowners. Our growing season is long, stretching roughly from March through October for warm-season grasses, but the intensity of growth varies dramatically throughout that period.

March Through May: Spring Growth Surge

As soil temperatures rise above 65 degrees in Metro Atlanta, warm-season grasses like Bermuda and Zoysia break dormancy and begin actively growing. March is typically a transition month where you might mow once or twice. By April, you should be on a regular weekly schedule. By May, when growth really accelerates, Bermuda lawns may need mowing every five to seven days, while Zoysia can usually hold at every seven to ten days.

Fescue, on the other hand, is in its prime during March through May. Spring is one of its peak growth periods, and you should expect to mow weekly, sometimes even more frequently after heavy spring rains combine with warm days.

June Through August: Peak Growing Season

This is when mowing becomes a serious commitment for Atlanta homeowners. Bermuda grass is growing at its absolute fastest, and you may need to mow every four to five days to maintain the proper height. Zoysia growth peaks as well, requiring mowing every seven days. The long daylight hours, intense heat, and afternoon thunderstorms create perfect growing conditions for warm-season turf.

Fescue reverses course during summer. Growth slows substantially, and in the hottest weeks, you may only need to mow every two to three weeks. Some fescue lawns barely grow at all during July and August, especially in full sun. Do not be alarmed if your fescue lawn looks thin or stressed during this period; it is a normal response to heat, not a sign of disease or neglect.

September Through October: Fall Transition

Warm-season growth begins slowing in September as days get shorter. Bermuda and Zoysia gradually reduce their mowing needs from weekly to every ten to fourteen days by late October. This is also the period when these grasses start transitioning toward dormancy.

For fescue, fall is the second peak growth period. September and October are the ideal months for overseeding, and newly seeded or established fescue will be growing vigorously. Expect to mow weekly through this period.

November Through February: Dormancy Period

Most warm-season grasses in Atlanta are fully dormant by late November and will not need mowing again until spring. The grass turns brown, but this is completely normal and healthy. Do not try to force growth during dormancy with fertilizer or excessive watering.

Fescue may continue to need occasional mowing through November and even into December during mild winters. Growth is slow, and you may only need to mow once or twice a month, but do not neglect it entirely. A final mow in late November at the proper height sets the lawn up well for winter.

The One-Third Rule Explained

The one-third rule is the single most important mowing guideline, yet it is the one most frequently violated by homeowners. The rule states: never remove more than one-third of the grass blade height in a single mowing.

Why does this matter? When you cut more than one-third of the blade, you cause significant stress to the plant. The grass essentially goes into shock. It diverts energy from root growth to blade recovery, which weakens the overall root system. The exposed lower portions of the grass blade, which are typically pale because they have not been receiving direct sunlight, are suddenly exposed to intense Georgia sun, causing scalding and browning. The cut creates larger wound surfaces that are more susceptible to fungal infection.

Here is what the one-third rule looks like in practice. If you maintain your Bermuda lawn at 1.5 inches, you should mow before it exceeds 2.25 inches. For a Zoysia lawn at 2 inches, mow before it reaches 3 inches. For fescue at 3.5 inches, mow before it reaches about 5.25 inches.

This rule is exactly why lawn mowing frequency in Atlanta needs to increase during peak growing season. When Bermuda is growing an inch or more per week, you cannot maintain the one-third rule with weekly mowing alone. You either need to mow more frequently or accept a slightly higher lawn. Most professionals opt for more frequent mowing.

If you have fallen behind on mowing and the grass is significantly overgrown, do not try to bring it back to the ideal height in one pass. Gradually lower the height over two or three mowings, removing no more than one-third each time. This is more work in the short term but far better for the long-term health of your lawn.

Recommended Mowing Heights

Here is a quick-reference table showing the recommended mowing heights for the most common grass types in Metro Atlanta:

Grass Type Ideal Height Mow When It Reaches Peak Season Frequency
Bermuda Grass 1 - 1.5 inches 2 - 2.25 inches Every 4-7 days
Zoysia Grass 1.5 - 2.5 inches 2.5 - 3.5 inches Every 7-10 days
Tall Fescue 3 - 4 inches 4.5 - 5.25 inches Every 7 days (spring/fall)
St. Augustine 3 - 4 inches 4.5 - 5.25 inches Every 7-10 days

Keep in mind that these are general guidelines. Lawns in heavily shaded areas should generally be maintained at the higher end of the recommended range, as taller grass blades capture more sunlight. Lawns in full sun can be maintained at the lower end. If your lawn receives heavy foot traffic, keeping it slightly taller provides more cushion and resilience.

Also note that seasonal adjustments are important. During summer heat stress, raise your mowing height by half an inch to provide the grass with additional protection. Many professional services in Alpharetta and Woodstock adjust mowing heights throughout the year for exactly this reason.

Common Mowing Mistakes Atlanta Homeowners Make

After years of lawn care experience across Metro Atlanta, we have identified the most common mowing mistakes that homeowners in Smyrna, Woodstock, and Alpharetta make. Avoiding these errors will dramatically improve your lawn's health and appearance.

Mistake 1: Mowing on a fixed schedule regardless of growth rate. Many homeowners mow every Saturday no matter what. But your lawn does not grow at a consistent rate. After a week of heavy rain and warm temperatures, it may need mowing after five days. During a dry spell, it might not need mowing for ten days. Let the one-third rule dictate your schedule, not the calendar.

Mistake 2: Scalping the lawn. Scalping means cutting the grass so short that you expose the soil or the stems below the leaf blades. This is incredibly stressful for the turf and creates an open invitation for weeds. Some homeowners intentionally scalp their lawns in early spring to remove dead material, which can be beneficial for Bermuda in a controlled way, but scalping during the growing season is always harmful.

Mistake 3: Mowing wet grass. After Atlanta's frequent summer thunderstorms, it is tempting to mow the next morning while the grass is still wet. Resist this urge. Wet grass clumps together, leading to uneven cuts. The clippings form heavy mats that smother the turf underneath. Wet conditions also make it harder for the mower blades to cut cleanly, resulting in torn grass tips that turn brown.

Mistake 4: Bagging all clippings. Unless your lawn is diseased or the clippings are excessively long, leave them on the lawn. Grass clippings decompose quickly and return valuable nitrogen and other nutrients to the soil. This natural recycling can reduce your fertilizer needs by up to 25 percent. Modern mulching mowers cut the clippings into fine pieces that settle into the turf and break down within a few days.

Mistake 5: Using dull mower blades. We already covered this, but it bears repeating because it is so common. A clean cut heals faster and looks better. A ragged tear from dull blades turns brown within hours and creates entry points for lawn diseases. If the tips of your grass blades look shredded or white rather than cleanly cut, your blades need sharpening.

Mistake 6: Mowing in the heat of the day. Mowing during the hottest part of a Georgia summer afternoon is stressful for both you and your lawn. The grass loses more moisture through the fresh cuts when temperatures are at their peak. If possible, mow in the early morning after dew has dried or in the late afternoon when temperatures start to cool.

Spring in Metro Atlanta is an exciting time for homeowners who take pride in their lawns. As temperatures rise and the landscape shakes off the gray of winter, your yard enters a critical transition period that sets the tone for the entire growing season. Getting your spring lawn care right in Atlanta is the difference between a lawn that thrives all summer and one that struggles from the start. This guide covers everything you need to know about spring lawn preparation in Georgia, from the first pre-emergent application to mulching your beds for the season ahead.

Whether you live in Acworth, Canton, Vinings, or anywhere else across Metro Atlanta, the spring lawn care checklist that follows is designed specifically for our region's climate, soil conditions, and common grass types. The timing of each task is calibrated to our local weather patterns, so you can follow this guide with confidence that you are doing the right thing at the right time.

When Does Spring Start for Atlanta Lawns?

In the Atlanta area, "spring" for your lawn does not follow the calendar. While the vernal equinox falls on March 20th, your grass operates on a different schedule based on soil temperature rather than air temperature. Soil temperature is a far more reliable indicator of when biological processes activate in your lawn, and it is what determines the true start of the growing season.

For warm-season grasses like Bermuda and Zoysia, the magic number is 65 degrees Fahrenheit in soil temperature. When the soil four inches below the surface consistently reaches this temperature, these grasses break dormancy and begin actively growing. In a typical Metro Atlanta spring, this happens somewhere between late March and mid-April. However, this can vary by a few weeks depending on the specific microclimate of your property. A south-facing lawn in Vinings may warm up two weeks before a shaded, north-facing lawn in Canton.

Cool-season fescue, on the other hand, starts its spring growth much earlier. Fescue begins growing when soil temperatures reach 50 degrees, which typically occurs in late February or early March in our area. By the time Bermuda is just waking up, fescue has been actively growing for a month or more.

You can monitor soil temperature with an inexpensive soil thermometer from any garden center, or you can check the University of Georgia's online soil temperature maps, which provide real-time data from weather stations across the state. This data is particularly valuable for timing your spring lawn care applications in Atlanta.

Here is a general timeline for spring lawn care in Metro Atlanta:

  • Late February: Pre-emergent herbicide application window opens
  • Early to Mid-March: Pre-emergent deadline for most of Metro Atlanta; fescue begins active growth
  • Late March to Early April: Warm-season grasses begin breaking dormancy; first mowing opportunities
  • Mid-April to May: Full spring growth mode; fertilization, mulching, and planting season

Pre-Emergent Herbicide Application

If there is one spring lawn care task you absolutely cannot skip, it is the pre-emergent herbicide application. This is your primary defense against crabgrass, goosegrass, and other summer annual weeds that will otherwise invade your lawn. The timing window is narrow, and once it passes, you have missed your opportunity for the year.

In Metro Atlanta, the pre-emergent window typically falls between mid-February and mid-March. The exact timing depends on soil temperature. You want to apply pre-emergent when soil temperatures at a four-inch depth are approaching 55 degrees Fahrenheit, which is the threshold at which crabgrass seeds begin germinating. Applying too early can result in the product breaking down before the germination window closes. Applying too late means crabgrass seeds have already sprouted, and pre-emergent herbicides do not kill existing plants.

For homeowners in Acworth and Canton, which sit at slightly higher elevations than other parts of Metro Atlanta, soil temperatures may warm up a few days later than in Vinings or Smyrna. Pay attention to your specific location rather than following a one-size-fits-all date.

The most common pre-emergent active ingredients available to Atlanta homeowners include:

  • Prodiamine (Barricade): Long-lasting and effective, prodiamine provides four to six months of control with a single application. It is safe for use on Bermuda, Zoysia, and established fescue lawns.
  • Dithiopyr (Dimension): Unique among pre-emergents because it also offers early post-emergent control of crabgrass that has just begun to germinate. This makes it a good choice if you think you may have missed the ideal application window by a week or two.
  • Pendimethalin (Pendulum): Widely available in granular form at home improvement stores. Effective but has a shorter residual activity than prodiamine, which means you may need a second application in late spring.

One critical note: if you plan to overseed fescue in the spring (though fall is strongly preferred), do not apply pre-emergent herbicide to those areas. Pre-emergent does not distinguish between weed seeds and desirable grass seeds. It will prevent your fescue seed from germinating just as effectively as it prevents crabgrass.

Dethatching and Aeration

Spring is an excellent time to address two common problems that plague Metro Atlanta lawns: thatch buildup and soil compaction.

Thatch is the layer of dead and living organic material that accumulates between the grass blades and the soil surface. A thin layer of thatch (half an inch or less) is actually beneficial because it insulates the soil, retains moisture, and provides a cushion for foot traffic. However, when thatch exceeds half an inch, it becomes a problem. Thick thatch prevents water, air, and nutrients from reaching the roots, creates a haven for insects and disease organisms, and can cause your lawn to become spongy and uneven.

Bermuda and Zoysia lawns are particularly prone to thatch buildup because of their aggressive spreading growth habits. If you can push your finger into your lawn and feel a thick, spongy layer before hitting soil, you likely have a thatch problem. The best time to dethatch warm-season grasses is in late spring (May) when the grass is actively growing and can recover quickly. Dethatching a dormant or semi-dormant lawn can cause severe damage.

Aeration addresses soil compaction, which is a universal issue in Metro Atlanta thanks to our heavy clay soil. Compacted soil restricts root growth, impedes water infiltration, and reduces the exchange of gases between the root zone and the atmosphere. Core aeration uses a machine to pull small plugs of soil from the lawn, creating channels that allow air, water, and nutrients to penetrate deeper into the root zone.

For warm-season grasses, the ideal aeration window in Atlanta is April through June, when the turf is actively growing and can fill in the holes quickly. For fescue, fall aeration (September to October) is preferred because it coincides with the grass's peak growth period. However, spring aeration of fescue can be done in March if the lawn has severe compaction issues.

If you live in a newer subdivision in Acworth or Canton where construction equipment has heavily compacted the soil, aeration is especially important. New construction sites often have subsoil compacted to the point where grass roots cannot penetrate more than an inch or two. Annual aeration for the first three to five years after construction is highly recommended.

The First Mow of the Season

The first mow of the season is a milestone moment for Atlanta homeowners. For Bermuda lawns, many professionals recommend a scalp mow in early spring, cutting the grass lower than the normal maintenance height to remove dead brown material and allow sunlight to reach the soil surface. This warms the soil faster and promotes earlier green-up.

A Bermuda scalp mow should be done in late February to mid-March, before the grass begins actively growing. Set your mower to its lowest cutting height (around 0.5 to 1 inch) and remove as much of the brown dormant material as possible. Bag the clippings during this mow rather than mulching them, as the heavy layer of dead material can smother new growth. After scalping, you should be able to see some soil between the grass runners.

For Zoysia, a light scalp mow is also beneficial but should not be as aggressive as Bermuda. Lower the mowing height by about half an inch below the normal maintenance height and bag the clippings. Zoysia recovers more slowly than Bermuda, so a severe scalp can take weeks to fill back in.

For fescue, do not scalp. The first mow should simply be at the normal maintenance height of 3 to 4 inches. Since fescue is already growing by late February in Metro Atlanta, your first mow may be earlier than you expect. Check the lawn regularly and mow when the blades exceed 4.5 to 5 inches.

Before the first mow of any season, always perform basic mower maintenance: change the oil, replace or clean the air filter, install a new spark plug, and most importantly, sharpen or replace the mower blades. Starting the season with sharp blades ensures clean cuts from day one.

Spring Fertilization Schedule

Spring fertilization is an area where many Atlanta homeowners go wrong. The instinct is to load up on fertilizer as soon as the weather warms up to "jump start" the lawn. But over-fertilizing in spring, particularly with warm-season grasses, can actually create more problems than it solves.

For Bermuda and Zoysia, wait until the grass has been actively growing for three to four weeks before applying the first fertilizer. This means mid-April at the earliest for most Metro Atlanta lawns, and May is often the better choice. Fertilizing too early forces top growth before the root system has fully reactivated from dormancy, resulting in weak, shallow roots that cannot support the lawn through summer stress.

The first spring application for warm-season grasses should be a balanced fertilizer with an N-P-K ratio close to 3-1-2 (for example, 15-5-10 or 18-6-12). Apply at a rate of about 0.5 to 1 pound of actual nitrogen per 1,000 square feet. If your soil test indicates adequate phosphorus levels, you can use a fertilizer with little or no phosphorus in the middle number.

For fescue, spring fertilization requires a lighter touch. Apply a modest amount of nitrogen in early March, no more than 0.5 pounds of actual nitrogen per 1,000 square feet. Heavy spring fertilization of fescue promotes excessive blade growth that the root system cannot sustain during the coming summer heat. Many lawn care professionals in Vinings and Canton recommend skipping spring fertilization for fescue entirely and focusing the heavy applications in fall instead.

Regardless of grass type, always water fertilizer in immediately after application. A good soaking within 24 hours washes the granules off the blades and into the soil where they can be absorbed by the roots. Fertilizer granules sitting on wet grass blades can cause chemical burns.

Pollen Season Preparation

If you have lived in Metro Atlanta for even one spring, you know about pollen season. The yellow-green dusting that coats everything from mid-March through late April is primarily pine pollen, and it is so heavy in some years that it looks like a layer of paint on every outdoor surface. While pine pollen is a nuisance, it does not cause the allergic reactions most people associate with spring allergies (that credit goes to oak, birch, and grass pollen), but it does have implications for your lawn and landscape.

Heavy pollen accumulation on your lawn is generally harmless. Rain washes it into the soil where it decomposes naturally and actually adds a small amount of nutrients. However, if pollen accumulates in thick layers during a dry period, it can temporarily block sunlight from reaching the grass. A light watering or the next rain shower solves this problem.

Where pollen preparation really matters is for your hardscape and outdoor living areas. If you have a deck, patio, driveway, or outdoor furniture, pollen season means regular cleaning. Pressure washing surfaces before and after peak pollen season keeps everything looking fresh. This is one of the reasons many homeowners in Acworth and Canton bundle lawn care with pressure washing services.

For homeowners with allergies, spring lawn care tasks like mowing and edging can be particularly unpleasant during pollen season. Wearing an N95 mask while doing yard work can help, but an even better solution is to hire a professional lawn care service to handle spring maintenance during the peak pollen weeks. Your lungs will thank you.

Irrigation System Startup

If your property has an in-ground irrigation system, spring startup is an important task that should not be rushed. Metro Atlanta does not typically experience the hard freezes that require full winterization (blowouts), but some freeze damage can still occur in particularly cold winters, especially in the northern areas like Canton and Acworth.

The spring startup process should include:

  1. Visual inspection: Walk the entire system and check for visible damage. Look for broken or tilted sprinkler heads, cracked pipes near the surface, and signs of erosion around heads that may indicate a leak.
  2. Controller check: Turn on your irrigation controller and verify the clock, day, and programming are correct. Replace the backup battery if needed. Review your watering schedule and adjust for spring conditions, which typically require less irrigation than summer.
  3. Zone-by-zone test: Run each zone manually and observe every head. Check for proper coverage, look for heads that are not rotating correctly, identify clogged nozzles, and note any areas receiving too much or too little water.
  4. Head adjustment: Adjust spray patterns so water is landing on the lawn and landscape beds, not on driveways, sidewalks, or the side of the house. Wasted water is wasted money, and overspray onto hardscape creates moss and slip hazards.
  5. Rain sensor test: Verify that your rain sensor or smart controller weather integration is functioning. These devices prevent your system from running during and after rainfall, saving significant water over the course of the season.

For spring watering in Metro Atlanta, start conservatively. Early spring typically brings enough rainfall to keep lawns adequately hydrated without supplemental irrigation. Set your system to run once or twice a week at most during March and April, and increase frequency only as temperatures rise and rainfall decreases in late spring.

Mulch Refresh and Bed Preparation

Spring is the traditional time to refresh mulch in your landscape beds, and for good reason. Fresh mulch improves the appearance of your entire property, suppresses weed growth, retains soil moisture, and regulates soil temperature as the growing season kicks in.

In Metro Atlanta, the most popular mulch options are:

  • Hardwood mulch: The most common choice. It breaks down over one to two years, adding organic matter to the soil. Dark brown or black-dyed hardwood mulch is popular in Vinings and Acworth for its clean, upscale appearance.
  • Pine straw: A Southern classic. Pine straw is lightweight, easy to spread, and acidifies the soil slightly, which benefits acid-loving plants like azaleas, camellias, and blueberries. It is also typically less expensive than hardwood mulch and readily available across Georgia.
  • Pine bark nuggets: Available in mini, medium, and large sizes. Pine bark is long-lasting and works well on slopes because it interlocks and resists washing away during heavy rain.

Apply mulch two to three inches deep, but keep it pulled back from the base of trees, shrubs, and your home's foundation. The common practice of "volcano mulching" around trees, where mulch is piled several inches high against the trunk, is one of the most damaging mistakes in landscape maintenance. It traps moisture against the bark, promotes disease, attracts insects, and can eventually kill the tree.

Before laying new mulch, remove any weeds that have emerged in the beds and apply a pre-emergent granular product to the bed surfaces. This prevents new weeds from germinating through the fresh mulch layer and keeps your beds looking clean much longer.
